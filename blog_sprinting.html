Sprinting: The Disease and the Cure

How funny is it that sprinting causes hamstring injuries?

But here’s the crazy part — in order to avoid getting hamstring injuries from sprinting... you have to sprint.

This is where coaching comes in.

Sprinting is both the disease and the cure. At the right dosages, sprinting doesn’t just make you faster and stronger — it makes you more robust. It builds the tissue capacity you need so you don’t get hurt from sprinting in the first place.

Picture this: you’ve been training for two weeks, playing small-sided games, but no matches. There’s a weekend off. Then suddenly, you’re in a game, you make a line break, you hit top speed... and bang — the sniper gets your biceps femoris long head. You’re out for six weeks.

Easily avoidable.

Very simply put, you need to sprint with a plan. For field sport athletes, the general recommendation is:

5–15 max velocity sprints per week

150–350 metres total, spread across the week (including the game)

And here’s the key — the sprinting has to be full intensity output. That means racing the clock, or racing your fastest teammate. With the right rest periods, your likelihood of injury drops dramatically.

Now you’re stronger, fitter, faster, and more robust — all from doing the very thing most people are afraid to do: sprint
