Mindset, Stoicism & Athletic Performance

Learn how practising Stoicism and cultivating a resilient mindset can transform your training and life.

Mindset, Stoicism, and Athletic

Performance

There’s a lot of information out there around mindset, wellness, and mindfulness. And look — it all comes from a good place. But as a practicing Stoic and performance coach, I find that Stoicism cuts through the noise and gives me concrete practices that genuinely improve my life.

So why is Stoicism so important for mindset? And why does it make you a better athlete, helping you get better results?

Consistency Compounds

Ultimately, if you are consistent, time is your biggest ally. Time compounds. The weeks, months, and years will pass either way. But if you’re taking action, building habits, and sticking with them, that consistency creates a snowball effect.

Seneca put it well: “Each day acquire something that will fortify you against poverty, against death, indeed against other misfortunes as well; and after you have run over many thoughts, select one to be thoroughly digested that day.”

In other words, cultivate one thing every day that moves you forward. A day can pass in the blink of an eye — so ask yourself: when I woke up and when I went to bed, did I move the needle even slightly closer to where I want to go?

And the feeling when you know you did — when you’ve been doing it day in, day out, week after week, month after month — you become a different person. You become someone who, no matter what life throws at you, is always moving forward. Some days it’s a lot. Some days it’s just a little. But you’re never standing still.

Complaining Solves Nothing

Stoicism doesn’t allow you to complain. Marcus Aurelius put it simply: “How unfortunate it happened? No. How fortunate it happened to you and you remain unharmed.”

Life will always throw setbacks your way. Unless your arm was literally chopped off, you’re okay. Don’t waste energy complaining — not even to yourself.

Picture this: your gym session is normally at 6 pm. Today you’re delayed, and it won’t be until 7. You’re tempted to skip it. The Stoic mindset kicks in: no one is coming to save you. No one else is going to achieve your goals. So you stop complaining, and you get on with it.

The Pursuit of Arete

Stoicism also drives consistency through the concept of arete — meaning excellence, or fulfilling your potential. The idea is simple: when you’re born, you have a ceiling of potential. When you die, you want to get as close to that ceiling as possible.

Arete is powerful because it’s free from comparison. You’re not competing with the person beside you or behind you. You’re competing with yourself, maximizing what’s in your control.

It’s similar to the Japanese idea of kaizen — small, steady improvement, 1% better each day. As Epictetus said: “Devote the rest of your life to making progress.” Not in a hedonistic, “burn the candle at both ends” way like a Jeff Bezos or Elon Musk. But with discipline. Discipline in all things. Even moderation.

Why This Matters for Athletes

If you want to improve athletic performance, get in shape, or simply become better, caring for your body is one of the best starting points. Consistency with your sleep, nutrition, and training

doesn’t just improve your fitness. It carries over. It helps you become more consistent in your work, in your relationships, in your studies — in life.

The key is this: focus on improving yourself, not proving yourself. Turn up for you. Be accountable to you.
